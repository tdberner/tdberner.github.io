title: Haverford College Senior Thesis
description:  The global water shortage crisis requires innovative and sustainable solutions for capturing water in arid regions. 
Sorbent-assisted atmospheric water harvesters (AWHs) using metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) and super-hygroscopic polymer films (SHPFs) 
have demonstrated potential in capturing water from low-humidity environments. We aim to explore the viability of titanium dioxide (TiO2) 
nanostructures as active components in electrically controlled AWH systems. Using the unique properties of TiO2 nanoneedles, applying an 
electric field to these nanostructures may be able to facilitate field-assisted water desorption. We plan to use a quartz crystal microbalance 
(QCM) to measure change in mass from field-assisted desorption, as well as a prototype system with a high-precision humidity sensor. Successful 
growth attempts have been made on pure titanium foil samples, with a thick forest of nanoneedles covering the surface of the foil. Although 
these results are promising, the next step is to measure their adsorption/desorption capabilities via quartz crystal microbalance and our prototype system.
